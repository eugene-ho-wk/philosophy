About

I am a PhD student in the Department of Philosophy at New York University, where I joined in 2023. I am also currently affiliated with Nanyang Technological University under the HIPS scheme.

My primary research concerns propositional attitudes, attitude reports, content, intentionality, and imagination. I am also interested in hyperintensional semantics and its applications to our accounts of imaginative engagement with impossible fictions, and to the question of what we can know through fiction and the imagination.

Further interests include non-classical logics, quantified modal logics, free logics, Meinongianism, haecceitism, essence, truthmakers, abstract objects, abstraction principles, social metaphysics, metaethics, error theory, non-cognitivism, fictionalism, constructivism, speech acts, conditional attitudes, indexicals, demonstratives, anaphora, philosophy of time, and early modern philosophy.

Before NYU, I completed a B.A. (Hons.) and an M.A. in philosophy at the National University of Singapore. You can reach me at eh3268@nyu.edu.

Research

  • An object-centric solution to Edelberg's puzzles of intentional identity

    Synthese 200 (5): 364  ·  2022

    Object-centric accounts of intentionality explain the aboutness of thought in terms of the intentional object the thought is about, holding that thoughts share a common focus in virtue of being about the same object. The paper addresses a puzzle raised by Alexander Sandgren: can two thoughts share a focus if their object does not exist? The paper defends an object-centric solution to Edelberg's intentional identity puzzles, arguing that the threat to such accounts—whether they posit abstract, merely possible, or Meinongian objects—can be deflected.

  • Knowledge Attributions, Contextualism, and Invariantism Manuscript

    PhilArchive  ·  2022

    This paper examines whether the knowledge norm of assertion, as defended by Timothy Williamson, supports Epistemic Contextualism over Subject Sensitive Invariantism. By comparing how each view handles Russell and Doris' indifference cases, the paper argues that the norm of assertion gives us reason to prefer Contextualism.
